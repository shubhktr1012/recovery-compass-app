# Recovery Compass Brand and Logo Brief

## Document Purpose

This brief is intended to give a logo designer enough strategic, product, and
emotional context to create a new identity for **Recovery Compass**.

This is a **new logo project**, not a logo refresh. The current logo is being
retired. The designer should not treat the existing mark as a base, reference,
or constraint.

This brief was synthesized from the product repo, in-app copy, onboarding flow,
program structure, and current UI system. It is meant to provide a strong
first-principles brief even before a separate founder interview or formal brand
strategy workshop.

## Brand Overview

Recovery Compass is a mobile recovery and behavior-change product designed to
help users break compulsive patterns and regain control over daily behavior.

The current product focus is **smoking cessation**, with adjacent signals toward
**alcohol reduction**, and broader platform ambitions across recovery,
nervous-system regulation, sleep, energy, and health-related self-regulation
programs.

At its core, Recovery Compass is not trying to be a generic habit tracker. It is
trying to be a structured recovery system that helps people move from:

- autopilot to awareness
- craving to interruption
- chaos to steadiness
- shame to self-respect
- negotiation to clear action

The brand promise is not dramatic transformation theater. It is calm,
intelligent, repeatable guidance that helps people find their way back to
self-command.

## What the App Actually Does

Recovery Compass is a guided program app, not just a content library.

It combines:

- structured day-by-day recovery programs
- guided audio sessions
- educational and reflective content
- journaling
- progress tracking
- trigger awareness
- financial and behavior projections
- crisis / SOS interruption support
- purchase and upgrade flows around guided recovery programs

The product helps users understand their triggers, personalize a recovery path,
and complete daily steps in sequence. It is built around progression,
reinforcement, and calm intervention rather than entertainment, social
performance, or aggressive motivation.

## Product Flow

### 1. First Impression / Onboarding

The app opens with a premium, calm onboarding sequence built around three core
ideas:

- navigate the urge
- structured freedom
- your compass

The language frames the app as a way to master cravings, regulate the nervous
system, and follow a guided path back to control.

### 2. Sign-In and Personalization

After onboarding, the user enters account flow and then a deep personalization
sequence. This is one of the most important pieces of product context for the
designer because it shows that the brand is not generic wellness. It is highly
specific, structured, and behaviorally informed.

The personalization flow asks about:

- what the user wants to quit or control
- preferred language
- name and age
- quit history / past attempts
- key triggers
- root causes behind the habit
- physical toll
- mental toll
- daily consumption and daily spend
- strongest primary motivation

The app then uses those answers to create a more tailored emotional and
practical frame for the journey.

### 3. Educational Reframing

During personalization, the product also educates the user. It explains that the
problem is not simply weak discipline or a moral failure. The copy frames the
loop as chemical, emotional, and environmental, and presents Recovery Compass as
a system for interrupting and redesigning that loop.

This matters for identity work because the brand is not punitive. It is serious,
but it is not scolding. It replaces shame with clarity and structure.

### 4. Projection and Commitment

The app calculates personal upside using the user’s own inputs, including
projected savings, avoided units, and 90-day progress framing. It makes the cost
of autopilot visible and gives the user a more concrete reason to commit.

This is an important part of the brand: Recovery Compass is emotional, but it is
also measured, rational, and practical.

### 5. Daily Home Experience

Once inside the app, the home screen functions like a recovery dashboard.

It includes:

- a greeting and sense of personal continuity
- a 90-day projection summary
- time in motion
- units avoided / progress signals
- a reminder of why the user started
- a clear CTA into today’s step

The tone is steady and respectful. It feels like a private recovery companion,
not a loud productivity tool.

### 6. Program Experience

The app’s core content is delivered as structured programs. The current visible
programs include a short reset and a longer quit path, with additional future
programs indicated for adjacent health categories.

Users move through day-based progression, with:

- a current day
- locked and unlocked content states
- daily titles and summaries
- estimated time
- audio when available
- text sections for guidance
- optional reflection prompts
- completion states

The experience is sequential and intentional. The brand should therefore feel
directional, stable, and designed around movement through a path.

### 7. In-Day Content Experience

A typical day contains:

- a day title and summary
- long-form guided content
- calming audio or meditation support
- an optional journal prompt
- a gentle closing message
- a completion action to mark progress and move forward

The tone across these screens is notably calm, low-drama, and psychologically
regulated. The app is trying to reduce internal friction, not add intensity.

### 8. SOS / Panic Interruption

The app includes an SOS mode for high-pressure moments. It gives the user an
immediate interrupt sequence with simple grounded actions like delaying the
decision, grounding the body, moving physically, and journaling the trigger.

This is one of the clearest signals of what the brand is: a calm intervention
system in moments of vulnerability.

### 9. Journal and Reflection

The app includes daily journaling with fields for mood, craving intensity, and
reflection. The role of journaling here is not performative self-expression. It
is practical self-observation and self-regulation.

### 10. Profile, Progress, and Purchase Status

The profile experience reinforces:

- days in motion
- savings projection
- triggers and questionnaire snapshot
- reason for starting
- current program access
- completed days
- upgrade / restore flows

This makes the brand feel structured, premium, and accountable without becoming
clinical or cold.

## Core Brand Promise

Recovery Compass helps people interrupt destructive autopilot and return to
steadiness, agency, and self-trust through structured daily guidance.

If the brand had to make one clear promise, it would be:

**We help you stay oriented when you would normally spiral.**

## Brand Positioning

Recovery Compass sits in a distinctive space between:

- recovery support
- premium wellness
- guided behavior change
- nervous-system regulation
- structured self-mastery

It should not feel like:

- a playful consumer habit tracker
- a sterile clinical medical app
- a macho “fight your addiction” brand
- a fluffy spiritual self-help product
- a cheap motivational app

The strongest position is likely:

**calm, premium, intelligent recovery guidance**

## Target Audience

From the product and copy, the current audience appears to be adults trying to
quit smoking and regain control over compulsive daily loops. The onboarding also
suggests potential expansion into alcohol-related behavior change.

The product appears to lean toward users who value:

- structure
- self-respect
- privacy
- measurable progress
- emotional control
- practical outcomes

The current onboarding and pricing cues suggest a likely **India-relevant market
context**, including English/Hindi language support and INR-based financial
framing.

The current copy sometimes leans masculine in tone, but the visual identity
should ideally be broad enough to support future expansion and not trap the
brand inside a narrow gender expression unless that is a deliberate strategic
choice later.

## Brand Personality

Recovery Compass should feel:

- calm
- grounded
- private
- intelligent
- premium
- steady
- composed
- warm without being soft
- serious without being severe
- supportive without sounding therapeutic in a generic way

The product does not sound loud, playful, rebellious, hyper-technical, or
motivational-guru driven. It sounds disciplined, quiet, and clear.

## Emotional Territory

The identity should evoke:

- clarity under pressure
- orientation in chaos
- a return to center
- calm control
- guided progress
- self-trust
- internal steadiness
- quiet strength

This is an important nuance: the brand is not about adrenaline, intensity, or
conquest. It is about regaining direction and reducing the mental noise that
drives relapse and autopilot.

## Strategic Brand Idea

The strongest conceptual territory for Recovery Compass is likely some
combination of:

- orientation
- return
- steady direction
- signal through noise
- path and progression
- clarity
- reset
- inner alignment

The name allows for compass-related interpretation, but the logo **does not need
to be a literal compass**.

In fact, a too-literal compass could become generic, outdoorsy, tactical, or
app-icon cliché. A more abstract or concept-led interpretation may be stronger
if it still communicates orientation, guidance, steadiness, or directed
recovery.

## Logo Objective

Create a new logo system for Recovery Compass that feels strategically correct
for a premium recovery product and scalable for a broader wellness / recovery
platform.

The logo should communicate some combination of:

- guidance
- recovery
- calm control
- trust
- structure
- progress
- orientation

It should be distinctive enough to own, simple enough to scale, and emotionally
aligned with the app’s quiet confidence.

## What the Designer Should Understand

This brand is built around a very specific user state:

- overwhelmed
- negotiating with themselves
- stuck in a loop
- tired of relapse
- craving control but not wanting judgment

Recovery Compass is not meeting that person with loud branding. It is meeting
them with composure, dignity, and a sense that there is a reliable path through
the moment.

That emotional starting point should shape the identity.

## Visual Direction

The visual system should likely aim for:

- restrained rather than loud
- premium rather than mass-market
- grounded rather than glossy
- clean rather than sterile
- calm rather than sleepy
- modern rather than trendy

Possible visual concept territories:

- directional systems
- alignment
- center of gravity
- return to self
- pathfinding
- signal and focus
- tension resolving into clarity
- movement from fragmentation into steadiness

The mark can be abstract, symbolic, typographic, or hybrid. A strong
wordmark-plus-symbol system is likely the safest route, but a powerful
standalone mark that scales to app-icon use is essential.

## Color Direction

The current product uses deep forest greens, sage tones, soft neutrals, and a
“warm luxury” atmosphere. That suggests the brand already leans toward calm
authority and restorative trust rather than bright tech energy.

This should be treated as strategic context, not a hard lock.

A designer should likely explore a palette that feels:

- grounded
- premium
- restorative
- credible
- warm-neutral rather than icy

Deep green is a strong candidate because it can communicate steadiness, healing,
and composure while differentiating from the overused blue of medical and
finance brands.

That said, the identity should work in **one color first**. If the mark does not
hold in black, white, or a single solid tone, it is probably not strong enough
yet.

## Typography Direction

The current product language pairs elegant serif energy with modern sans-serif
support. That combination creates a useful strategic clue:

- the brand wants refinement
- but it also wants clarity and usability

The logo typography should therefore avoid looking:

- too clinical
- too startup-generic
- too delicate
- too ornamental
- too aggressive

The typographic voice should feel composed, intelligent, and premium.

## What to Avoid

Avoid:

- literal broken-cigarette symbolism
- obvious rehab clichés
- hospital / medical cross iconography
- military or “battle mode” aesthetics
- overwrought spiritual symbolism
- generic compass clichés
- outdoor adventure branding
- overcomplicated emblems that fail at app-icon size
- low-cost self-help aesthetics
- logos that feel narrow to smoking if the product expands

Also avoid identities that look like they belong to:

- fintech
- crypto
- hiking / outdoor gear
- meditation-only apps
- productivity SaaS

## Practical Requirements

The logo system should include:

- primary logo
- secondary lockup
- standalone symbol
- app icon adaptation
- one-color version
- reverse / light-on-dark version
- small-size legibility guidance
- clear spacing guidance

The symbol should work across:

- mobile app icon
- splash and onboarding
- dashboard header or badge moments
- website / landing page
- social profile image
- email and PDF materials
- merchandise or print if needed later

## Success Criteria

A successful identity for Recovery Compass should make someone feel:

- this is trustworthy
- this is calm
- this is serious
- this is premium
- this is not judging me
- this knows what it is doing
- this can guide me through something difficult

It should also feel extensible enough that the brand could move from smoking
cessation into a broader recovery and regulation ecosystem without needing
another full identity reset.

## Suggested Deliverables to Request From the Designer

- logo concept presentation with rationale
- primary logo
- alternate lockups
- standalone mark
- favicon / app icon adaptation
- monochrome and reversed variants
- color specifications
- typography recommendation if applicable
- SVG, PNG, PDF, and source vector files
- a short usage guide

## Working Summary

Recovery Compass is a premium recovery app for people trying to break harmful
loops and regain calm control over their lives. It personalizes a user’s path,
guides them through structured daily programs, supports them in high-pressure
moments, and reinforces progress with a private, steady, nonjudgmental tone.

The logo should express that same promise.

Not chaos. Not hype. Not moralizing. Not generic wellness.

The identity should feel like orientation, steadiness, and trustworthy progress
in moments when a person is most likely to lose their way.
